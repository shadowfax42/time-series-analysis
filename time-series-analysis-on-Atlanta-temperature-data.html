Using the 20 years of daily high temperature data for Atlanta (July through October, (file temps.txt))

We first read our data into a tibble, then select all but the DAY column. We then convert our dataset into a vector of temperatures by concatenating all of the columns. We will then apply ts() to our vector. Since each year in our data has 123 days, we will set the frequency to 123 and the start parameter to 1996

# Create a time series object of the temps data
temps_ts <- as_tibble(temps) %>%
    select(., -DAY) %>% unlist() %>% as.vector() %>% ts(start = c(1996, 1), frequency = 123)

Decomposing Time Series¶

decompose() let us estimate the trend, seasonal and random components of our temperature data

In [4]:
# Decompose the time series
myts_decomposed <- decompose(temps_ts)
# myts_decomposed
In [5]:
# Plot the decomposed time series
plot(myts_decomposed)

  • The plot above shows the original time series (top), the estimated trend component (second from top), the estimated seasonal component (third from top), and the estimated irregular(randomness or noise) component (bottom). We see that the estimated trend component shows an increase around 2011 then a gradual decrease around 2013 - 2014 followed by another increase around 2015

  • By looking at the seasonal component of our decomposed time series, we see that the seasonality across 20 years are identical. This can be misleading because of how seasonality is being calculated in the decompose() function. The decompose() function computes seasonality by averaging, for each time unit, over all periods.

  • We can adjust our original time series by removing the random/noise component from it and then apply decompose() on the adjusted time series
In [6]:
# Remove the random component from our series
temps_ts_no_random <- temps_ts - myts_decomposed$random
plot.ts(temps_ts_no_random)
In [7]:
# decompose the time series without the random component
temps_ts_no_random_decomp <- decompose(temps_ts_no_random)
plot(temps_ts_no_random_decomp)

Here, we can see the biggest increase in the trend component at around 2013 where summer lasted more than 100 days. Seasonality still looks the same as before.

Another way of clearky exposing seasonality is by de-trending our time series. This will results into a new series

In [8]:
# De-trending our time series
temps_ts_deTrend <- temps_ts - myts_decomposed$trend
plot(as.ts(temps_ts_deTrend))

It looks like de-trending our original time series didnt help us see the seasonality of our data.

Build a HoltWinters model¶

We will use the original time series to build our exponential smoothing model.

  • We will inspect single, double and triple exponential smoothing

The output of HoltWinters() are alpha = 0.839 and a = 63.3. The alpha parameter is closer to 1 which implies that our series doesnt have much randomness. With less random fluctuations in our system, we can trust the current observation. This means that today's baseline is closer to the observed data. In our case, the baseline is a = 63.3

Conclusion:¶

Using 3 different types of exponential smoothing to fit our temperature data and having an alpha closer to 1, and both beta and b almost equal to zero, we can conclude that Atlanta temperatures from July to October over the period of 1996 and 2015 have no trend.

We also plotted the seasonal component of the triple exponential smoothing model, and all of the years have identical seasonality. This gives us the confidence to say that we dont see a pattern in the data that would indicate whether or not an unofficial end of summer has gotten later over the 20 years.
